## What Citizens United Got Wrong

The US Supreme Court's 2010 decision in *Citizens United v. FEC* held that corporations have First Amendment rights equivalent to natural persons — that spending money is a form of protected speech, and that limiting corporate political spending limits free expression.

The philosophical basis: corporations are associations of persons, and associations of persons have the same rights as individual persons.

This reasoning has a fatal flaw that the dissent identified and history has confirmed:

> *"Corporations have no consciences, no beliefs, no feelings, no thoughts, no desires. Corporations help structure and facilitate the activities of human beings, to be sure, and their 'personhood' often serves as a useful legal fiction. But they are not themselves members of 'We the People.'"*
> — Justice John Paul Stevens, dissenting

**The empirical result:** Within 14 years of Citizens United, corporate dark money routed through low-disclosure jurisdictions had become the dominant force in US elections. The human voter did not gain a voice. The human voter was priced out of the conversation — not by force, but by scale.

Liberland's corporate tax haven model replicates this dynamic in the fiscal domain. The lesson of Citizens United is not that corporations deserve rights. It is that when you give entities with unlimited capital and no mortality the same standing as humans, **the humans lose.**

## The Libertarian Case Against Corporate Personhood

The libertarian tradition that Liberland claims as its foundation has never fully reconciled the corporate form with natural rights theory. Here is why it cannot:

**A corporation is a creature of the state.**

The defining feature of the modern corporation is limited liability — the legal doctrine that shareholders cannot be held personally responsible for the corporation's debts, harms, or legal judgments beyond their invested capital. This doctrine does not exist in nature. It was created by government statute, beginning in the mid-19th century, specifically to encourage capital formation by reducing investor risk.

Without that government grant, there are no corporations. There are only partnerships — where every owner is personally, fully, and jointly liable for everything the entity does.

This means:

1. The corporation exists only because the state said it could.
2. The corporation's most valuable property — limited liability — is a government subsidy that caps downside for capital while exposing workers, creditors, and communities to the full downside.
3. A libertarian who champions corporate personhood is championing a state-granted privilege, not a natural right.

**If you are a genuine libertarian, the corporation should be your most suspicious institution — not your most protected one.**

## The Homestead Principle and Birthright TIME

The objection most frequently raised by libertarian-aligned thinkers is that birthright TIME constitutes redistribution — taking from the productive to give to the unproductive.

This objection misidentifies the mechanism.

**Birthright TIME is a homestead claim.**

The homestead principle — foundational to libertarian property theory — holds that a person acquires rights to unowned resources by mixing their labor with them. The paradigmatic case is the settler who clears and farms land: the labor invested creates a prior claim.

Every human being has already homesteaded something before any market existed to evaluate it: **their own time.** You arrived before the market. The market did not create you. Your time — the irreplaceable, non-renewable substrate of all human labor — is your prior claim.

TIME Protocol makes this claim legible on-chain. It is not redistribution. It is recognition of what was already true: that existence precedes exchange, and that a human being's time has value before any employer or market has evaluated it.

The Bitcoin reserve backing birthright TIME is not funded by taxation. It is funded by the protocol's transaction fee revenue — the same fee that makes the clearinghouse work. The issuance is not a transfer from the rich to the poor. It is a creation event, backed by protocol-generated value.
